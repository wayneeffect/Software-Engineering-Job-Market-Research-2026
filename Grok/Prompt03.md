**Entry-level jobs in software engineering** are roles designed for recent graduates, career changers, or those with little to no professional experience (typically 0–2 years). These positions focus on learning while contributing to real projects under supervision.

### Common Entry-Level Job Titles
Here are the most frequent titles you'll see in postings:

- **Junior Software Engineer** / **Software Engineer I**
- **Associate Software Engineer**
- **Entry-Level Software Developer**
- **Software Development Engineer (SDE) I** (common at big tech like Amazon)
- **Full-Stack / Front-End / Back-End Developer (Junior)**
- **Quality Assurance (QA) Tester** or **Test Engineer**
- **Software Engineer – New Grad** / **Early Career**

Other related starting points:
- Technical Support Specialist
- Web Developer
- Application Developer
- DevOps / Site Reliability Engineer (junior roles)

### Typical Responsibilities
In these roles, you’ll usually:
- Write, test, and debug code for features or bug fixes.
- Participate in code reviews and agile ceremonies (standups, sprint planning).
- Collaborate with senior engineers, product managers, and designers.
- Work on documentation, testing (unit/integration), and basic maintenance.
- Learn the codebase, tools, and company practices.

### In-Demand Areas for Entry-Level Roles
- **Full-stack or web development** (React, Node.js, Python/Django, Java/Spring, etc.)
- **Backend development** (APIs, databases, cloud services)
- **Frontend** (modern JavaScript frameworks)
- **Mobile** (iOS/Android with Swift/Kotlin or cross-platform)
- **QA / Testing** and automation
- Emerging: AI-assisted development, cloud (AWS/Azure), basic DevOps

Government contractors, defense (e.g., General Dynamics, Leidos), and traditional companies often have more stable entry-level pipelines than pure big tech.

### Requirements and Qualifications
Most roles expect:
- Bachelor’s degree in Computer Science, Software Engineering, or related field (preferred but not always required — bootcamps/self-taught can work with strong portfolios).
- Proficiency in at least one programming language (e.g., Python, Java, JavaScript, C#).
- Understanding of data structures, algorithms, Git, and basic software design.
- Personal projects, internships, open-source contributions, or a strong GitHub portfolio.
- Soft skills: Problem-solving, communication, eagerness to learn.

### Salary Expectations (US, 2026)
- **Range**: Often $60k–$120k+ depending on location, company, and role.
- New grad / true entry-level at tech companies: Frequently $90k–$150k+ total compensation (base + bonus + stock) in high-cost areas.
- Lower in non-tech hubs or smaller companies; government/contractor roles may offer stability and benefits.

### Market Context
The entry-level market is competitive due to high numbers of graduates, AI productivity tools, and selective hiring. However, opportunities exist, especially with targeted applications, networking, and demonstrated skills. Many companies post "New Grad" or "2026 Graduate" roles.

**Tips to Land One**:
- Apply aggressively via LinkedIn, Indeed, company career pages, Handshake, and new-grad job lists.
- Build projects relevant to the role (e.g., full-stack apps, contributions to open source).
- Prepare for technical interviews (LeetCode, system design basics).
- Consider rotations or apprenticeship-style programs at larger firms.

Entry-level roles are a launchpad — focus on gaining experience, mentorship, and rapid learning to progress to mid-level within a few years. The long-term demand for software engineers remains strong.
